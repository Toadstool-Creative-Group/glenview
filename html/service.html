    Services

    We offer different types of services to cater to our patients needs!

      × ❮ ❯
    • Comprehensive Eye Examinations

      Our comprehensive vision exams include a determination of prescription and a thorough evaluation of visual function including eye alignment, color vision, depth perception, focusing skills, eye teaming, along with a health check of the front and back of the eye to determine changes related to dry eye, glaucoma, macula degeneration, high blood pressure and diabetes.

    • Contact Lenses

      Contact lenses are medically prescribed to provide vision correction and maintain eye health. We carry an extensive collection of lenses for all conditions and ages. We have nationally recognized experts in the use of specialty contacts to manage eyes damaged from disease or trauma.

    • Corneal Refractive Therapy

      Contact lenses may be used to mold the surface of the eye via overnight wear which provides vision correction without the need for spectacles or contacts during the day.

    • Evaluation, Management, and Treatment of Eye Disease

      Our office has the ability to detect and manage eye disease such as red eye , allergy eyes, diabetes, glaucoma, keratoconus, trauma, cataract, abrasions to the eye and styes. We specialize in the management of dry eye.

    • LASIK Evaluation

      Lasik is a surgical procedure to correct vision so that spectacles or contacts are not required for most activities. To qualify for lasik, the eye must not have any surface eye disease, be thick enough to correct the prescription and have normal shape. Once these factors are evaluated a referral can be made to the appropriate corneal surgeon to have the procedure performed with follow up visits performed at our office.

    • Low Vision Services

      For individuals who have limited vision due to eye disease, low vision services work to maximize the function of the remaining vision. This may include special spectacles, magnifiers, telescopes, along with aids for daily living.

    • Sports Vision

      Sports vision is the art and science of providing vision care for the athletic population. The extended exam is designed to make sure that limitations in the visual system are not interfering with athletic performance. A sports vision exam includes the evaluation of sharpness of sight, distance depth perception, eye movements, vision processing speed, eye hand coordination, eye teaming and focusing accuracy.

    • Vision Therapy

      Many individuals have problems with reading skills, using both eyes together, or maintaining eye focus or shifting focus for different distances. These deficiencies in the visual system can contribute to eye strain, headaches, and fatigue. Vision therapy entails a series of activities to improve eye movements, eye teaming, and eye focusing skills.

    • Visual Information Processing

      A visual information processing, or perceptual, disorder ( VIP) refers to a hindered ability to make sense of information taken in through the eyes. This is different from problems involving sight or sharpness of vision. Difficulties with visual processing affect how visual information is interpreted or processed by the brain.

    Laterality / Directionality

    • The Piaget Left-Right Awareness Test investigates problems in the areas of laterality and directionality. Laterality is the ability to identify the difference between left and right on one's self. Directionality is the ability to identify the difference between the left and right side of objects in space

      The Reversals Frequency Test evaluates the existence and frequency of letter and number reversals. In the Execution subtest, the patient is asked to write numbers and letters. This subtest assesses long-term memory (storage and retrieval).

      In the Recognition subtest of the Reversals Frequency Test, the patient is asked to discriminate between correctly oriented letters and numbers and their mirror images

    • Fine Motor / Eye-Hand Coordination

      The Detroit Test of Motor Speed and Precision is a fine motor coordination task requiring both speed and precision. The patient is given a page with a sequence of circles that gradually decrease in size. The patient is instructed to accurately place an x within as many circles as possible during the allotted time. A poor speed score indicates a decreased ability to use the small muscles of the hand efficiently. A poor precision score indicates a decreased ability of the eyes to direct the hand efficiently.

    • Visual-Motor Integration

      The Developmental Test of Visual-Motor Integration evaluates the ability to accurately reproduce a visual stimulus. In order to do this, the patient must be able to see that the pattern is made up of a finite number of parts and that these parts interrelate in a very specific manner. To reproduce the pattern, the patient must integrate this information with fine motor skills to generate a motor response.

    • Visual Discrimination

      The Visual Discrimination Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to match one of four similar forms exactly to a stimulus form.

    • Visual Memory

      The Visual Memory Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to remember for immediate recall (after four or five seconds) all of the characteristics of an abstract stimulus form and to find it among four similar forms after the stimulus is removed.

      The Visual Sequential-Memory Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to remember for immediate recall (after four or five seconds) a series of known forms and to identify that specific sequence from among four similar sequences.

    • Spatial Orientation

      The Visual-Spatial Relations Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to determine from among a series of forms of identical configuration, the one single form or part of a form that is turned in a different direction from the others.

    • Form Constancy

      The Visual Form Constancy Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to find an individual form when it is presented as smaller, larger, rotated, reversed, or hidden among other forms.

    • Figure-Ground Discrimintation

      The Visual Figure-Ground Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to identify a given form when hidden within a picture with background distractions.

    • Visual Closure

      The Visual Closure Subtest of the Test of Visual Perceptual Skills evaluates the ability to recognize an entire form when only portions of the form are presented.
